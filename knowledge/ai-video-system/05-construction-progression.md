# AI Construction Video System — Construction Progression

## Purpose

Redesign the build progression for hidden-house videos.

The narrative is not about discovering a pre-existing cave.

The narrative is about gradually creating a hidden dwelling through believable construction progress.

## Core Narrative Rule

The viewer should feel:

"Someone is slowly creating this hidden place."

Not:

"Someone found a finished cave."

## Construction Realism Rules

- The build should feel achievable by a single person over time.
- Every stage must be physically connected to the previous stage.
- Each stage should increase construction progress without skipping too far ahead.
- Excavation volume must grow gradually.
- Scale must remain believable for one person working over time.
- Materials should appear only when the stage logically supports them.
- Environmental consistency is more important than decorative detail.

Avoid:

- Instant large chambers
- Impossible excavation volume
- Sudden luxury interiors
- Unrealistic scale jumps
- Finished rooms appearing too early
- Architectural spaces before excavation is believable
- Visible construction markings that cause aggressive model inference

---

# 6-Stage Hidden-House Progression

## Stage A — Natural Untouched Environment

## Viewer Perception

The viewer sees a beautiful untouched natural location.

There should be no obvious sign that a hidden dwelling will be built.

## Visual Objective

Establish the environment as natural, remote, and desirable.

The location itself must be strong enough to serve as the opening hook.

## Allowed Changes

- Natural waterfall, cliff, cave wall, forest, mountain, tree, or abandoned exterior
- Natural mist, moss, vegetation, terrain, water, shadows, and weather
- Cinematic composition
- Photorealistic detail

## Forbidden Changes

- Entrances
- Tunnels
- Caves that imply access
- Dark openings
- Construction markings
- Tools
- Workers
- Artificial structures
- Doors, windows, lights, beams, or furniture

## Nano Banana Risks

- Adding a cave or opening too early
- Adding artificial paths, stairs, lights, railings, bridges, or ruins
- Making the location too fantasy-like
- Weakening the environmental hook

## Validation Criteria

- Environment feels untouched
- No hidden entrance is visible
- No construction intent is visible
- Composition is beautiful and photorealistic
- Scale and camera are clear enough to preserve across later stages

---

## Stage B — Subtle Anomaly

## Viewer Perception

The viewer senses something unusual but may not understand it immediately.

Most viewers should still perceive the natural environment as the main subject.

## Visual Objective

Introduce a hidden possibility without making it the focal point.

The anomaly should be subtle, partial, and visually secondary.

## Allowed Changes

- Small geological recess
- Natural dark shadow
- Slight crack in rock
- Small hidden gap near the edge of the waterfall or cliff
- Partial obstruction by mist, vegetation, rock, or water

## Forbidden Changes

- Large cave opening
- Clear doorway
- Centered entrance
- Visible room
- Construction markings
- Artificial structures
- Lights
- Doors
- Beams
- Tools or workers

## Nano Banana Risks

- Turning the anomaly into the main focal point
- Centering the opening
- Making the opening too large
- Making the opening look like a finished cave or doorway
- Revealing an interior too early

## Validation Criteria

- Natural environment remains dominant
- Anomaly is visible only on closer inspection
- Opening, if present, is small and partially obscured
- No room or chamber is visible
- No artificial construction is visible

---

## Stage C — Early Excavation

## Viewer Perception

The viewer understands that someone has just started digging.

The hidden dwelling does not exist yet.

## Visual Objective

Show the first clear construction progress while keeping the opening small and primitive.

This stage should communicate effort, not completion.

## Allowed Changes

- Slightly deeper rough recess
- Freshly broken rock texture
- Small rock chips
- Damp debris
- Uneven shallow floor
- Opening barely large enough for one adult to crouch into

## Forbidden Changes

- Chamber
- Room
- Interior reveal
- Architectural space
- Large tunnel
- Support beams
- Doors
- Lighting fixtures
- Furniture
- Finished surfaces
- Luxury elements

## Nano Banana Risks

- Skipping directly to a cave chamber
- Revealing a room
- Enlarging the opening too much
- Creating a pre-existing cave discovery narrative
- Adding architectural features or lighting

## Validation Criteria

- Opening remains small
- A person could barely enter
- Only shallow excavation is visible
- No chamber or room exists
- Viewer feels: "Someone has just started digging"

---

## Stage D — Expanded Excavation

## Viewer Perception

The viewer sees that the hidden dwelling is being actively created over time.

The space is becoming usable but is still raw.

## Visual Objective

Expand the shallow excavation into a rough, human-scale work cavity without making it feel finished.

## Allowed Changes

- Deeper tunnel
- Slightly wider work cavity
- More broken rock debris
- Rough floor beginning to level
- Clearer interior depth
- Raw volcanic rock walls
- Minimal temporary construction traces

## Forbidden Changes

- Large finished chamber
- Luxury interior
- Furniture
- Polished floors
- Wall panels
- Glass
- Decorative lighting
- Finished architecture
- Large multi-room spaces

## Nano Banana Risks

- Creating a huge cave volume
- Jumping to a habitable room too early
- Adding artificial architecture
- Losing scale relative to the original opening
- Disconnecting from the exterior environment

## Validation Criteria

- Excavation is larger than Stage C but still raw
- Scale feels achievable by one person over time
- Space remains rough and unfinished
- Waterfall/cliff identity remains connected
- No luxury or finished room elements appear

---

## Stage E — Habitable Unfinished Space

## Viewer Perception

The viewer understands that the excavation is becoming a future dwelling.

The space is not luxurious yet, but it is starting to become usable.

## Visual Objective

Introduce basic habitability while preserving unfinished construction realism.

## Allowed Changes

- Leveled rough floor
- Basic waterproofing
- Minimal structural reinforcement
- Simple rough work surfaces
- Early utility channels without decorative lighting
- Dry zones or cleared living footprint
- Natural rock still dominant

## Forbidden Changes

- Finished luxury furniture
- Beds, sofas, kitchens, shelves, decor
- Glass walls
- Premium wood finishes
- Polished surfaces
- Warm final reveal lighting
- Generic apartment layout

## Nano Banana Risks

- Jumping to final luxury too early
- Overbuilding with beams, panels, stairs, or modern walls
- Losing the natural rock identity
- Making the space look like a generic interior

## Validation Criteria

- Space feels usable but unfinished
- Natural rock remains dominant
- Construction progress is visible
- No final living furniture appears
- Scale remains plausible

---

## Stage F — Completed Hidden Dwelling

## Viewer Perception

The viewer receives the payoff.

The hidden dwelling now feels real, desirable, and earned.

## Visual Objective

Deliver the final reveal with strong contrast against Stage A.

The result should feel like a completed hidden residence integrated into the original natural location.

## Allowed Changes

- Warm layered lighting
- Premium furniture
- Wood
- Glass
- Stone integration
- Soft textiles
- Refined storage
- Finished floor
- Natural rock preserved as part of the design

## Forbidden Changes

- Sci-fi bunker look
- Fantasy palace
- Generic apartment with no natural context
- Removing all rock identity
- Losing waterfall/cliff connection
- Unrealistic scale expansion

## Nano Banana Risks

- Making the final image too generic
- Losing the waterfall context
- Over-polishing materials
- Creating fantasy architecture
- Making the room too large or impossible

## Validation Criteria

- Final dwelling feels desirable
- Natural location remains visible or strongly implied
- Rock/waterfall identity is preserved
- Luxury feels grounded and photorealistic
- The transformation from Stage A feels earned

---

# Production Guidance

Use this 6-stage progression when a 4-stage workflow skips too much narrative logic.

For Nano Banana:

- Use larger steps than a 15-scene construction workflow.
- Use smaller steps than a 4-stage reveal workflow when the narrative needs construction credibility.
- Avoid language that implies the model should discover a pre-existing cave.
- Prefer language that suggests gradual hand-made progress.

Best framing:

Natural environment

→ Subtle anomaly

→ Early excavation

→ Expanded excavation

→ Habitable unfinished space

→ Completed hidden dwelling
